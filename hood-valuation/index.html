1Executive Summary

At $110, Robinhood is priced as a platform that will compound earnings at 20%+ for years without interruption — and our probability-weighted fair value is ≈$87 (range $32 bear / $78 base / $160 bull), roughly 20% below the market. The bull thesis is not wrong about the company; it is aggressive about the price. Robinhood has done something genuinely impressive: it broke its dependence on the crypto cycle. In the quarter just reported, crypto revenue halved year-over-year — the same −47% collapse that gutted Coinbase's consumer line — and Robinhood's total revenue still grew 15%, because four other engines (options, event contracts, net interest, and Gold subscriptions) more than filled the hole. That is the achievement the stock is celebrating. Our disagreement is narrow and specific: the current price already banks the celebration and several years of the next act. To justify $110, an investor must underwrite roughly a 20% compound earnings growth rate, sustained through a full crypto winter and a Fed easing cycle, with the single fastest-growing revenue line resting on contested legal ground. We think that is the bull case, not the base case — and we weight it accordingly.

The Current State of Robinhood's Story

Robinhood is a brokerage, but it is more useful to think of it as an attention-and-balance-sheet machine. It has built one of the most engaged young customer bases in American finance — about 27 million funded accounts holding roughly $307 billion — and it monetizes that base three different ways at once. First, it earns fees when customers trade: options, stocks, crypto, and now event contracts (bets on real-world outcomes). Second, it earns interest on the cash and securities those customers leave behind — margin loans, idle cash, securities lending. Third, it sells a subscription, Robinhood Gold, that a growing share of customers now pay for. The genius of the model is that each new thing to trade is a nearly costless addition to the same base: once you have the customer, offering them options, then crypto, then prediction markets costs almost nothing and drops almost entirely to the bottom line.

Data shows the machine is working and, importantly, diversifying. A year ago crypto was roughly 27% of revenue and the market treated Robinhood as a crypto-and-meme proxy that would live or die with Bitcoin. Today crypto is about 13% of revenue, and the business grew right through the coin's 50% drawdown. We believe this is real and the market is right to re-rate the company out of the pure-crypto bucket — that is why Robinhood trades near $110 while Coinbase sits 63% below its high. Our caution is about magnitude, not direction. At $110 the stock carries roughly 53 times trailing earnings — and that headline understates the true multiple, because last year's earnings were flattered by a 10.7% tax rate that will not persist; on a normalized tax rate the multiple is closer to 62 times. A number like that is an explicit forecast: it says this company will grow into it, fast, and keep growing. Our job is to ask what has to be true for that forecast to pay, and to weigh it honestly against the outcomes where it doesn't.

The strongest argument against our caution deserves a fair hearing, and we give it one in Section 8: Robinhood keeps inventing new high-margin revenue lines. Event contracts went from a rounding error to 10% of revenue in twelve months. If the company attaches a new vertical like that every year or two — banking, advisory, tokenized assets, international via the Bitstamp acquisition — then any fixed forecast of its earnings is too low by construction, and the stock is cheap. That is a coherent view. But it asks the buyer to pay today, in full, for verticals that do not yet exist, while the one that does exist and matters most (event contracts) sits in front of a Supreme Court petition due in weeks.

It is no longer, on the evidence, a claim on the crypto price: crypto is ≈13% of revenue and falling as a share.
  • The repricing has already happened — upward. The easy money in the "Robinhood is not just a crypto stock" trade has been made.
  • Cycle position: BTC peaked ≈$126k in October 2025 and sits at $62.4k, −50%. Crypto trading revenue is in the trough; the question the valuation turns on is whether the other engines can carry a $100B valuation while crypto is quiet, and what happens to those engines when the Fed cuts.
  • Structural changes this cycle: the launch and rapid scaling of event contracts (prediction markets); the Bitstamp acquisition (closed June 2025, $224M cash — global/institutional crypto infrastructure); the Rothera/MIAXdx joint venture giving Robinhood its own CFTC-licensed exchange and clearinghouse; a credit card and banking push; and a demonstrated ability to hold operating margins near 47% while growing.

The one fact that carries the note. Crypto revenue fell 47% and total revenue rose 15%. In the 2022 winter, a comparable crypto collapse took Robinhood's whole business down with it; this time it did not. Event contracts alone ($3M → $104M) added more revenue than crypto lost. This is demonstrated diversification, not a promise of it, and it is the empirical core of the bull case. Our valuation accepts it fully; the disagreement is entirely about what to pay for it.

The three engines, and their very different qualities:

  1. Flow monetization (transaction revenue, ≈$623M/qtr). PFOF and spreads on options (the largest), crypto, equities, and event-contract fees. High-margin and cyclical, but expanding in surface area — each new tradable product is a near-costless attach to the same 27M customers. This is the compounding-platform engine.
  2. Balance-sheet spread (net interest, ≈$359M/qtr). Margin interest ($193M, +75%), interest on segregated cash and sweep, securities lending, and a fast-growing credit card ($32M, +220%). Steady and large — but the only engine with a macro headwind: Robinhood discloses that a 100bp move in rates shifts annual net revenue by ≈$294M pre-tax, and management explicitly warns Fed cuts will pressure this line.
  3. Subscription annuity (Gold, ≈$50M/qtr, +32%). 4.34M subscribers, +36% YoY. Small in dollars but strategically central: Gold drives deposit capture, higher ARPU, and retention — the flywheel that turns a trader into a primary-account relationship.

The key performance metrics (10-Q/10-K): 27.4M funded customers (+6% YoY); 29.1M investment accounts (+8%); Total Platform Assets $307.3B (+39%), of which equities $207.5B, crypto $30.5B (+11%), RIA-advised $42.6B, customer cash $41.6B; net deposits $17.7B in the quarter (≈22% annualized); ARPU $157 (Q1'26, +8% YoY) and $171 (FY2025, +40% YoY); Gold subscribers 4.34M.

What Robinhood Gold actually offers

Gold is a ≈$5/month (or ≈$50/year) membership, and understanding what it bundles explains why 4.34M customers now pay for a service whose competitors give the pieces away. The offering has evolved, but as of this writing it centers on turning cash and balances into yield and perks:

  • A higher yield on uninvested cash swept to partner banks (the Gold rate has run several hundred basis points above the non-Gold sweep rate — the single biggest draw, and highly rate-sensitive).
  • A retirement-contribution match (a 3% match on IRA contributions for Gold members vs 1% for non-members) and a deposit/transfer boost on money brought onto the platform.
  • A lower margin rate (a reduced APR on borrowed funds, with a small first tranche included) and larger instant deposits.
  • Professional-grade tools: Level II market data (Nasdaq TotalView) and third-party research (Morningstar).

The strategic point matters more than any single perk: Gold converts a commission-free trader into a paying, deposit-holding, primary-account relationship. It is the mechanism behind the rising ARPU and net-deposit numbers, and it is the clearest evidence for the "platform, not brokerage" thesis — a customer who pays a subscription and parks their cash is far stickier and more monetizable than one who only trades. (Specific rates and match percentages change with the rate environment and promotions; treat the categories above as the durable structure, not fixed figures.)

The business model's central controversy: payment for order flow

Robinhood pioneered zero-commission trading, and a fair question follows: if the customer isn't paying, how does the flow engine make money — and at whose expense? The answer is payment for order flow (PFOF). Robinhood routes customer orders to wholesale market makers (Citadel Securities and others), who execute them and pay Robinhood a rebate for the flow. This is the bulk of the transaction-revenue line, and it drives a real critique worth stating plainly:

  • The "you are the product" objection. Because the market maker — not the customer — pays Robinhood, the incentive is to route orders to whoever pays the most rebate, which is not necessarily whoever gives the customer the best price. "Commission-free" does not mean cost-free; the cost is embedded in the execution quality of the fill (a slightly worse price, or a slower fill), where it is invisible to the user.
  • This is not merely anecdotal. In December 2020 the SEC charged Robinhood and settled for $65 million, finding the firm had misled customers about PFOF and failed its duty of best execution — and that, even accounting for zero commissions, customers were collectively worse off by tens of millions of dollars on execution quality. PFOF has drawn repeated regulatory scrutiny since (the SEC has periodically floated proposals to restrict it or force order-by-order competition), and it remains a standing regulatory-and-reputational overhang, not a settled matter.
  • The counter-argument, for balance. PFOF is legal, disclosed, and standard across the zero-commission industry (Schwab, Fidelity's model differs but most retail brokers use it); retail orders routinely receive price improvement versus the public best bid/offer, and the wholesale-execution model genuinely lowered the all-in cost of trading for small investors. The honest debate is not "free or a scam" but whether the price improvement customers receive is as good as it could be if the incentive were aligned differently — a question on which the evidence is mixed and the disclosure is imperfect.

For valuation, PFOF sits in the risk stack two ways: as a regulatory tail (a ban or an order-competition mandate would compress the options/equities flow economics — see Section 8) and as a franchise-trust question (the model's optics are a recurring reputational vulnerability for a company whose brand is "democratized finance"). We do not haircut the base case for a PFOF ban — none is imminent — but a buyer should know the flow engine's economics rest on an arrangement that regulators have penalized once and continue to eye.

4Financial Statement Analysis & Quality of Earnings

We screened the statements against a standard earnings-quality checklist before valuing. The business is financially clean; the one issue that matters is a tax rate that flatters the reported multiple.

  • The reported P/E understates the true multiple. FY2025 net income was $1,883M ($2.05 diluted EPS) on a 10.7% effective tax rate — depressed by a −8.7pt share-based-compensation benefit and a −3.1pt R&D credit, with the prior-year valuation-allowance release already behind it. Normalizing to a ≈24% mid-cycle rate cuts FY25 EPS from $2.05 to ≈$1.75, and lifts the trailing P/E from ≈53× to ≈62×. FY2024's $1,411M net income was worse on this dimension — it carried a net tax benefit (−32.6% rate) from the DTA valuation-allowance release, so year-over-year EPS growth optically overstates operating progress. Anchor on normalized, not reported, earnings.
  • Fortress, and unusually clean, balance sheet. Corporate cash ≈$5.0B against no funded corporate debt (only undrawn revolvers). The ≈$45B total-asset base is customer and segregated assets, matched by liabilities — not corporate leverage. Net corporate cash ≈$5.2B ($5.64/share) is a real floor under the equity.
  • Provision for credit losses is rising ($24M → $36M/qtr) with the margin and credit-card books — worth monitoring but small and consistent with growth, not a red flag.
  • Dilution from share-based compensation runs ≈1.5–2%/yr; we value on 915M diluted shares.
  • The event-contracts line has no history through a stress event and rests on the legal outcome in Section 7 — an earnings-durability question, not an accounting one.
  • Auditor Ernst & Young; timely filings; no restatements. Robinhood's earnings question is durability and price, not integrity.

5Valuation

5.1 What model the business dictates

Robinhood is not one cash flow to normalize; it is three engines with different cyclicality, margin, and durability. And unlike Coinbase — which we value by normalizing a crypto cycle — Robinhood's earnings variance comes from growth durability, not the coin. So the workhorse is a six-engine revenue build to a normalized FY2028, capitalized at a justified multiple; the discipline is a reverse valuation that asks what the current price already assumes; and the floor is net corporate cash. We calibrate a fixed-plus-variable cost model to FY2025 (operating expense $2,379M on revenue $4,473M, ≈47% operating margin) and tax at 24%.

5.3 Reverse valuation — what $109.86 requires

Stripping out net cash, the market pays ≈$95.3B for the franchise. At a terminal 25×, that requires ≈$3.8B of normalized net income ($4.17 EPS); at 30×, ≈$3.2B. Against normalized trailing earnings of ≈$1.6B, those are compound growth rates of ≈24%/yr (at 25×) or ≈18%/yr (at 30×) sustained for four years — with no cyclical interruption, through a Fed easing cycle, and with the event-contracts line intact. It is achievable — Robinhood has grown faster than that in bursts — but it is a demanding, conjunctive forecast being priced as the base case. This is the single most important exhibit in the note: $110 is not a valuation, it is a growth commitment.

6HOOD Versus Direct Bitcoin Ownership

The question a crypto allocator should ask about Robinhood has changed. For Coinbase, the question is "does the equity beat the coin?" (it does not, in any scenario we modeled). For Robinhood, the question is now "is this even a crypto instrument anymore?" — and the answer is largely no.

  • Crypto is ≈13% of revenue at high incremental margin. Flexing Bitcoin across our scenarios (≈$40k / $62k / $110k) while holding everything else fixed moves Robinhood's fair value by only single-digit percentages — a fraction of Coinbase's ±60% swing. The crypto engine matters, but it no longer is the company.
  • Robinhood holds zero Bitcoin on its balance sheet — its exposure is pure flow monetization, not treasury. (Contrast: Coinbase 16,492 BTC; Block ≈9,000 BTC.)
  • The one vector that re-couples Robinhood to crypto is Bitstamp — global/institutional crypto infrastructure — but its revenue contribution was disclosed as immaterial.
  • The market's own verdict, in the data: Robinhood's rolling 90-day beta to Bitcoin peaked at ≈1.22 in April 2025 — when crypto was ≈27% of revenue — and has fallen to ≈0.88 as crypto halved to ≈13%. Robinhood's crypto beta is essentially a live readout of its crypto revenue share, and it is falling in real time. (See the synthesis note for the full three-name comparison.)

Verdict, the mirror image of our Coinbase conclusion: buying HOOD as levered crypto exposure is now largely a category error — but buying it while fearing crypto is no longer disqualifying, because the coin is only a modest part of what you own.

7Special Situation: The Event-Contracts Regulatory Showdown

The fastest-growing line in the model — event contracts, $3M → $104M in a year, now ≈10% of revenue — sits on contested legal ground, and its fate is the swing factor between our base and bear cases. Robinhood offers the contracts through Robinhood Derivatives, LLC (a CFTC-registered Futures Commission Merchant), cleared via CFTC-regulated venues (ForecastEx, Kalshi), and has begun vertically integrating by acquiring a CFTC-licensed exchange and clearinghouse (the Rothera/MIAXdx JV). The legal question is whether federal commodity law preempts state gambling regulators for sports-event contracts.

8Risk Factors (to this view — what makes our estimate too low, and too high)

What would make ≈$87 too low (the bull's case):

  1. The serial-vertical machine (highest-impact upside risk). If Robinhood attaches a new high-margin business every year or two — banking, advisory/Cortex, tokenized assets, international via Bitstamp — our FY2028 engine list is incomplete and every scenario understates revenue. This is the same "compounding-platform" risk our Coinbase note flagged, and Robinhood has more evidence for it (event contracts are the proof of concept). It is the best reason to own the stock.
  2. A faster crypto cycle. If BTC bottoms early and 2027 is a full bull year, the crypto engine re-inflates and a 34× multiple on $4B+ of earnings supports $160+. We weight this 25%; the market implicitly more.
  3. Take-rate durability. If the young base keeps trading actively as balances grow — Gold's success suggests willingness to pay rather than trade less — the monetization mean-reversion below may not bite.

What would make ≈$87 too high (our residual caution, and the bear):

  1. Monetization mean-reversion. Robinhood earns ≈1.5% of client assets vs Schwab's ≈0.35%. As the cohort ages and balances grow — the bull's own thesis — monetization per dollar tends to compress. Asset growth and take-rate decay partially cancel; terminal-state Robinhood on $1T of assets may earn far less than a linear extrapolation implies.
  2. The Fed. Net interest is the largest revenue line and rate-sensitive: ≈$294M pre-tax per 100bp. An easing cycle mechanically pressures it — usually alongside the risk-on conditions that help the flow engines, but not always in the same quarter.
  3. Event-contract legal reversal (Section 7) removes ≈10% of revenue and the fastest grower.
  4. Multiple risk. At 53–62× the valuation is acutely sensitive to sentiment; a growth stumble re-rates hard toward broker multiples (the bear).

Framework note. As with Coinbase, our disagreement with the market is about earnings (what Robinhood normally earns and how fast it can grow), not multiples (what the market pays). We hold a compounding-platform lens here — not the mean-reversion lens we applied to Coinbase — precisely because Robinhood's mid-winter revenue creation is direct evidence for it. The remaining gap to $110 is that even a generous platform view struggles to clear a 20%+ four-year growth commitment priced as the base case.

10Ten Open Questions, Answered Against the Model

1. Is Robinhood still a crypto stock? Largely no. Crypto is ≈13% of revenue and falling; fair value moves single-digit percentages across our BTC scenarios; the balance sheet holds no Bitcoin; and the market's own beta (peaked 1.22, now 0.88) tracks the shrinking crypto revenue share. It is a diversified retail-finance platform with a crypto option attached, not a crypto proxy.

2. Why is the real P/E higher than the 53× headline? Because FY25's 10.7% tax rate (SBC and R&D benefits, post valuation-allowance release) will not persist. Normalized at 24%, EPS falls from $2.05 to ≈$1.75 and the multiple rises to ≈62×.

3. What does $110 actually require? Roughly $3.2–3.8B of normalized net income within four years — an 18–24%/yr compound growth rate, uninterrupted, through a rate-cut cycle. Achievable but conjunctive; priced as the base case.

4. Can the diversification survive a Fed easing cycle? Partly. Net interest (≈$294M per 100bp) is the exposed line, but rate cuts usually accompany the risk-on conditions that lift options and crypto volumes. The net effect is a headwind to the steadiest engine, cushioned but not offset.

5. Do event contracts survive the courts? Uncertain and binary. A Third Circuit win favors the industry; a Ninth Circuit loss creates a split and a likely Supreme Court fight (NJ petition due Aug 4). Robinhood already exited Nevada sports contracts. We model the line probability-weighted, not at run-rate.

6. Where does monetization go as the base ages? Down per dollar (toward broker norms), up in aggregate (more assets). The two partly cancel; the bull needs asset growth to outrun take-rate decay, which is unproven at scale.

7. What is the balance sheet worth? ≈$5.2B net corporate cash, no funded debt — $5.64/share of hard floor, and optionality to fund buybacks or acquisitions.

8. Is crypto's 13% a trough or a decline? Cyclical trough in our base/bull (it re-inflates with the next BTC leg), structural decline only in the bear. Either way it is no longer the swing factor for the whole equity.

9. How real is the ARPU story? Real: ARPU reached $157 in Q1'26 (+8% YoY) and $171 for FY2025 (+40% YoY), on rising Gold penetration (4.34M, +36%) and a near-doubling margin book. The base is monetizing more per user even as it diversifies away from any single product.

10. What would change our mind toward the bull? A second and third "event-contracts-style" vertical scaling to material revenue; take-rate holding as assets compound; and the crypto trough proving to be a floor above which the other engines keep growing. If those appear, fair value migrates toward $110–140 without a crypto bull required — and we will update on the evidence.

11Conclusion

Robinhood has earned its re-rating: it broke the crypto dependency that defined it, and it did so in the middle of a crypto winter, on the strength of engines that did not exist or did not matter a cycle ago. That is a better company than the market gave it credit for two years ago, and our note is not a bear case on the business. It is a valuation discipline on the price. At $110 — ≈62× normalized earnings — the stock prices an uninterrupted 20% compounding machine as the base case, with its fastest-growing line awaiting a Supreme Court petition. Our probability-weighted fair value of ≈$87 says: pay for the platform Robinhood has proven, weight the platform it might become, and do not pay in full today for verticals that do not yet exist. The single cleanest signal to watch is not Bitcoin — it is whether the next new vertical scales the way event contracts did, and whether the courts let the last one survive. If Robinhood keeps inventing engines faster than its take rate mean-reverts, we are too conservative; until it proves the next one, ≈$87 is our estimate and $110 is a growth commitment we would not underwrite at par.
